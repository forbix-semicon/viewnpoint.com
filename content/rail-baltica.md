<p>Imagine stepping onto a train in Helsinki. You sip your coffee as the Finnish coastline glides by. A short ferry ride connects you to Tallinn. Then, without changing trains or changing gauges, you continue to Warsaw. Then to Berlin. Then to Paris.</p>
<p><strong>This is not a dream. This is Rail Baltica.</strong></p>
<p>And it is the most important infrastructure project you have never heard of.</p>
<hr class="section-break">

<h2>The Great Disconnect</h2>
<p>Here is a strange fact. The Baltic states of Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania are in Europe. But their trains do not run on European tracks.</p>

    <h3 class="table-title">Baltic Rail vs Mainland Europe</h3>

            </tbody>
        </table>
    </div>

<p>This is a legacy of Soviet occupation. A physical barrier in the ground.</p>
<p>Today, a train from Tallinn to Warsaw is a logistical nightmare. Passengers must disembark at the Polish border. Their luggage is unloaded. Their train wheels are changed—or they board a new train entirely. The delay? <strong>Up to a full day.</strong></p>
<p>For freight, this is death. For passengers, it is a deterrent. For Europe, it is an embarrassment.</p>
<hr class="section-break">

<h2>The Solution: One Track to Connect Them All</h2>
<p>Rail Baltica changes this. It is a new, fully electrified, standard-gauge railway. It will connect:</p>
<ul>
    <li>Helsinki (via ferry to Tallinn)</li>
    <li>Tallinn</li>
    <li>Pärnu</li>
    <li>Riga</li>
    <li>Kaunas</li>
    <li>Vilnius</li>
    <li>Warsaw</li>
</ul>
<p>From Warsaw, you are connected to the entire European network. Berlin, Vienna, Paris, Rome—all reachable on the same tracks.</p>

<h2>How It Controls Inflation (Yes, Really)</h2>
<p>Inflation has many causes. One of them is inefficiency.</p>
<p>When goods are stuck at borders. When trucks burn fuel for hours in traffic. When logistics costs rise—prices rise with them.</p>
