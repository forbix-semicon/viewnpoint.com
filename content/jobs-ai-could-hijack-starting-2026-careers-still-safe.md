<p>A friend who runs a small verification team in Bengaluru called last month. Half joking, half scared, he said: <em>"We are not being laid off. We are being automated in place."</em> His company still employs him. It just expects the same output with fewer people—and more Copilot.</p>

<p>That is the shape of AI and jobs in 2026. Not always a pink slip on Friday. Sometimes a hiring freeze that never ends. Sometimes a client who wants the same statement of work at 30% fewer hours. Sometimes a CEO on a podcast saying, with a straight face, that the firm needs <strong>"less heads."</strong></p>

<p>We wrote this piece—<strong>Senthil</strong> and <strong>Ian</strong> (Ian works as a <strong>Principal Cloud Architect at Oracle</strong>, where he sees enterprise buyers ask daily which roles they can trim after rolling out AI assistants)—because the headlines are loud and the spreadsheets are messy. Some jobs are getting hit hard <em>right now</em>. Others are safer than Twitter would have you believe. The trick is knowing <strong>which bucket yours falls into</strong>, and not hiding behind the vague label "IT."</p>

<h2>First, what the numbers actually measure</h2>

<p>One boring sentence that saves you from being misled: <strong>most layoff totals count announced cuts, not proven AI replacements.</strong> Companies file WARN notices, issue memos, and tell reporters why they are cutting. They also use "AI" when they mean offshoring, margin defence, or post-pandemic correction.</p>

<p>Still, the <em>direction</em> is real. The IMF warned in 2024 that <strong>almost 40% of global employment</strong> is exposed to AI—with higher shares in advanced economies. Employer surveys cited in mainstream news (including the World Economic Forum's 2025 outlook, as reported by Euronews and others) project heavy churn in clerical and routine digital work through 2030.</p>

<h2>Stop saying "IT is unsafe." Which IT jobs, exactly?</h2>

<p>Saying "technology jobs" without a role is like saying "medicine is stressful." True, and useless. Below we score <strong>job risk</strong> from automation and AI-led headcount pressure—not your worth as a human.</p>

<div class="risk-legend" aria-label="Job risk scale">
    <span class="risk-badge risk-very-low">Very low</span>
    <span class="risk-badge risk-low">Low</span>
    <span class="risk-badge risk-medium">Medium</span>
    <span class="risk-badge risk-high">High</span>
    <span class="risk-badge risk-very-high">Very high</span>
</div>

<ul>
    <li><strong>Very high</strong> — pattern repetition; juniors do bulk of work; AI already does a decent first draft.</li>
    <li><strong>High</strong> — headcount per project is falling; tools mature fast.</li>
    <li><strong>Medium</strong> — human judgment, hardware, or politics still gate decisions.</li>
    <li><strong>Low / very low</strong> — accountability, safety, scarce expertise, or physical world.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Country focus: USA, India, China</h2>

<h3>United States — the receipt printer is working overtime</h3>

<p>The U.S. is where CEOs most openly link cuts to AI—partly because Wall Street rewards the story. In spring 2026 alone: Oracle's estimated <strong>20,000–30,000</strong> global reduction to fund data centres; Meta's <strong>8,000</strong> cuts plus <strong>6,000</strong> unfilled roles scrapped; Microsoft's first broad buyout offer (~<strong>8,750</strong> eligible); Amazon's <strong>30,000</strong> corporate jobs gone since October 2025; Google's rolling Cloud and security trims.</p>

<p><strong>Higher job risk:</strong> tier-1 support, mortgage processing ops, content moderation farms, junior analysts, ad operations, routine SaaS implementation, generic full-stack roles.</p>
<p><strong>Lower job risk for now:</strong> licensed trades, critical infrastructure technicians, nurses, senior security incident leads, chip bring-up engineers, enterprise sellers with P&amp;L relationships, AI governance &amp; risk roles.</p>

<h3>India — the bench is the canary (beyond TCS)</h3>

<p>India's pain is not always announced as "AI layoffs." It shows up in net headcount, fresher intake, and restructuring names:</p>

<ul>
    <li><strong>TCS:</strong> −23,460 net FY26; ~12,000-person reduction programme completed.</li>
    <li><strong>Infosys:</strong> −8,440 in Q4 FY26; youngest cohort share at a 15-year low.</li>
    <li><strong>Cognizant:</strong> Project Leap — up to $270M severance; press estimates 7,000–15,000 roles globally, India-heavy.</li>
    <li><strong>Tech Mahindra:</strong> −1,993 in Q4 as margin transformation continues.</li>
    <li><strong>Oracle India:</strong> widely reported as a major share of Oracle's March 2026 global cuts.</li>
    <li><strong>Fresher pipeline:</strong> TCS guided ~25,000 campus offers for FY27 vs 40,000+ in peak years; active tech job openings hit a 28-month low in mid-2026.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Higher job risk:</strong> manual testing, AMS application support, voice BPO, body-shop maintenance projects, boilerplate Java/.NET teams without domain depth.</p>
<p><strong>Lower job risk:</strong> cybersecurity for global banks, chip verification leads, Oracle/SAP functional experts who own audit outcomes, on-site client architects, regulated healthcare IT.</p>

<h3>China — employment politics over efficiency</h3>

<p>Beijing will not cheer mass white-collar displacement. Policy discourse in 2025–2026 worries about AI shrinking labor's share of income. Expect friction on blunt layoffs even as factories automate.</p>

<p><strong>Higher job risk:</strong> gig delivery, warehouse work, low-end content production, basic customer chat, repetitive manufacturing inspection.</p>
<p><strong>Lower job risk:</strong> roles tied to state priorities (semiconductor self-sufficiency, EV supply chain), skilled technicians in advanced manufacturing, senior engineers in strategic sectors.</p>

<h2>What actually keeps you employed</h2>

<p>Not vibes. Not a LinkedIn certificate in prompt engineering. Four patterns show up again and again:</p>

<ol>
    <li><strong>Accountability.</strong> Someone still goes to jail, loses a license, or signs a regulatory filing. AI does not want that job.</li>
    <li><strong>Scarce context.</strong> You know the client's messy history, the fab quirk, the hospital's politics.</li>
    <li><strong>Physical reality.</strong> Solder smoke, patient weight, a vibrating bearing.</li>
    <li><strong>Compound skills.</strong> RTL + security. Nursing + Spanish + geriatrics. Sales + Oracle ERP + public sector procurement.</li>
</ol>

<p>Reskilling helps—but pick depth, not another shallow "AI tools" badge. News coverage of employer surveys keeps flagging <strong>analytical thinking, resilience, and technological literacy</strong> as the skills that still command a premium through 2030.</p>

<hr class="section-break">

<h2>The uncomfortable bottom line</h2>

<p>AI is not eating <em>all</em> jobs. It is eating <strong>tasks</strong>, then reorganizing headcount around what is left. Oracle's spring 2026 cuts—up to 30,000 in some estimates—landed the same week Meta and Microsoft trimmed thousands more. India felt it through TCS, Infosys, Cognizant, and Oracle India in the same quarter. Japan is importing robots because there are not enough humans. China is trying to automate without sparking unrest.</p>

<p>If you are early in a pattern-match career—testing, ticket closing, template UI, routine RTL, document review—the clock is louder than your manager admits. If you own outcomes in a regulated, physical, or politically complex domain, you have more runway than Reddit suggests.</p>

<p><strong>Neither of us thinks you should panic. We think you should stop pretending the word "IT" on your contract is a shield.</strong></p>
